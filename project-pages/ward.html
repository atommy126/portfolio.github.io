WARD

Summary

WARD is a 2D dungeon-crawler roguelike. You are the pilot of an armored suit, which has been designed to withstand the anomalous conditions created by an uncontained rift. Your suit is your lifeline, you must keep it powered to stay alive. As you descend to the bottom of the laboratory, you will find chips, the remnants of the lab's research. Each can modify your suit in a unique way, giving you the equipment boost needed to reach the bottom. You have but one directive: reach the bottom and close the rift.

Starting to Build

Before I started programming, I realized that I wanted a lot of control over different elements, so I decided to use the Monogame framework instead of a game engine. I was also interested in how different elements worked at their base level, so building them myself seemed like a good approach. My next step was getting buttons working, so the game had some amount of interactivity. After that, I began researching Monogame shaders that could give me some kind of CRT effect. I quickly found an open-source set of shaders and tweaked them to my liking. The next parts of development were largely the creation of new kinds of buttons, like radio buttons and toggle buttons. Development continued on like that for a while, where I was primarily implementing different elements that the game needed to function.

The Missing Piece

Near the end of May 2025, I had almost every vital menu implemented, as well as many of the important game mechanics, such as player map movement, combat, and the chip system. This was everything essential to the intended gameplay loop, but something felt off. I thought about it for a while, until I realized something very important about the game. It wasn't fun. It wasn't the lack of polish that was the problem, the game was missing something major. I thought about the balance of the game for a while, and decided that WARD's primary issue was a skill issue. The game had plenty of strategy and luck elements to it, which can make a perfectly fine game on their own. But WARD didn't have enough of either to account for zero skill elements. To fix that, I decided to add a new mechanic, sequences.

Sequences

Sequences are essentially minigames that the player must complete during combat. The number, type, and difficulty of sequences is impacted by various factors, such as the player's chips and enemies present. Most sequences are either pass or fail, though some have a spectrum of outcomes. For example, basic chips may increase damage done on a successful sequence, while another chip may add different damage types depending on the positions of different sliders. Enemies also have sequences that add an extra challenge for the player, such as regions that cause damage and visual distortion when moused over.

Chips

Chips are WARD's upgrade system. Each chip provides some kind of effect to the player, depending on type. Some chips are passive, while others require a completed sequence to have an impact. What makes chips interesting is their ordering, which can be changed at will outside of combat. Chips apply in a set order, depicted in the inventory by arrows. When I designed this system, I wanted the player to use strategy when deciding where to place chips. I plan to create chips that grant great benefits while imposing a cost, such as a chip that doubles one damage type while zeroing another. These mechanics add more thought to choosing upgrades, and the system is even more complex when unmovable corrupted chips are added into the mix.

Narrative

I also plan to include optional narrative sections in the form of logs and data entries. These small pieces of lore can be read to gather an understanding of who you are playing as and of what happened in the laboratory. Since these logs would be completely optional, they provide another goal for players who wish to solve the mysteries of the lab, while not being an obstacle for those who would rather focus on gameplay.
